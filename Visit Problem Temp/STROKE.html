Diagnosis
Definitions

Stroke: Rapid development of loss of cerebral function, most commonly with unilateral weakness or speech disturbance that lasts more than one day with no history of head injury.

TIA: Rapid development of loss of cerebral function most commonly with unilateral weakness or speech disturbance, that lasts less than one day with no history of head injury. Associated with ~5% risk of stroke in 48 hours, 15% risk of stroke in 3 months, 40% risk of major vascular event in 10 years.

In one quarter of patients, brief --minutes to less than one hour---cerebral or retinal warning signs and symptoms occur with both stroke and TIA before more long-lasting signs.

Causes

Hypertension, diabetes, cigarette smoking, atrial fibrillation, tobacco chewing, dyslipidemia, increased Lp(a), folic acid, homocysteine, valvular heart disease; and in women oral contraceptives, history of pre-eclampsia or eclampsia.

Types

Immediate investigation

If a very-likely cause of ischemic stroke, like an embolus from atrial fibrillation or known valvular heart disease, is not evident, then distinguishing between an ischemic event and a hemorrhagic event is critical, and this can be done with a head non-contrast CT scan. Making this distinction is critical, especially in cases of TIA, because immediate antiplatelet treatment with aspirin (+ with potentially other drugs-clopidogrel), significantly lowers the risks of further stroke or cardiovascular events in the following 48 hours, and over 3 months in particular.

Red Flags

Conditions which can mimic TIA include: migraine aura, peripheral vertigo, epilepsy (e.g., parietal-lobe epilepsy), hypoglycemia, transient global amnesia, and postural hypotension.

Rapid progression of neurological changes suggests a hemorrhagic event.
